    UV Photoresponsivity of GeO₂ Photodetectors

    Measuring the deep-UV spectral response of ultrawide-bandgap GeO₂ detectors with a tunable third-harmonic laser source — a collaboration with the Fu and Blair labs.

    Shadi Mirmohammadi — with Imteaz Rahaman and Botong Li (Dr. Kai Fu’s lab) and Prof. Steve Blair, Electrical & Computer Engineering, University of Utah

    The question

    Germanium dioxide (GeO₂) is an emerging ultrawide-bandgap semiconductor: the film used here has a bandgap of ~4.7 eV, corresponding to an absorption edge near 264 nm — deep in the UV, where sunlight doesn’t reach the ground. That makes it a natural candidate for solar-blind UV photodetectors. Dr. Kai Fu’s group fabricated metal–semiconductor–metal detectors on their GeO₂ films (interdigitated contacts, 250 × 300 µm active area) and confirmed they respond to a UV flashlight. The real test, though, is quantitative: how does the responsivity vary with wavelength across the band edge?

    The trick: making deep-UV light at any wavelength you want

    Tunable light at 230–280 nm doesn’t come out of a lamp. We generated it by third-harmonic generation (THG) of a tunable Ti:sapphire laser in Prof. Blair’s lab — my setup for UV-pumped photoluminescence, realigned for this experiment. Tuning the fundamental sweeps the third harmonic across ~233–280 nm, with the power at each wavelength calibrated on a Thorlabs power meter with a UV extension.

    At each wavelength we recorded dual-sweep I–V curves from −10 to +10 V in the dark and under illumination — waiting for the photocurrent to stabilize before each sweep, and logging laser power on the same schedule — so that responsivity could be computed as (Iphoto − Idark) / Pincident.

    What the detectors do

    With power calibration and area correction in place, the spectral response shows exactly the physics you’d want from a solar-blind detector: responsivity climbs steeply below ~255 nm toward the deepest measured wavelength (233 nm), and falls off approaching the ~264 nm band edge — a band-edge cutoff, not a resonance peak.

    The detectors also switch cleanly when the light is chopped, with the slow decay tail after each OFF step revealing persistent photoconductivity — a well-known trait of UWBG oxide films and one of the handles for judging film quality:

    My role

    I operated and aligned the THG deep-UV source, calibrated the optical power at each wavelength, and contributed the responsivity analysis — including the beam/detector-area correction and the interpretation of the spectral shape as a band-edge cutoff. Device fabrication and electrical measurements were led by Imteaz Rahaman and Botong Li in Dr. Kai Fu’s group; the measurement campaign is feeding into a publication in preparation.
