    Excitation-Switchable Eu³⁺ Luminescence in GdScO₃

    A nominally undoped crystal that changes its emission color with the excitation wavelength — and what its glow reveals about substrate quality.

    Seyedeh Shadi Mirmohammadi, Afrouz Taherian, Yunshan Wang, Diego P. Fernandez, Michael A. Scarpulla, Steve Blair, and Berardi Sensale-Rodriguez University of Utah

    Manuscript ready for submission to Optical Materials Express (2026).

    A crystal that isn’t supposed to glow

    GdScO₃ is a workhorse perovskite substrate for growing epitaxial oxide films — prized for its lattice match to titanates, not for its optics. The substrates we studied are commercial, nominally undoped single crystals from two different vendors (Stonylab (100) and CrysTec (110)). Shine deep-UV light on them, though, and they light up with bright, sharp visible emission.

    The culprit turned out to be europium at the parts-per-million level — an unintentional trace impurity, thousands of times below deliberate doping, roughly one Eu³⁺ ion per 10⁵–10⁶ Gd³⁺. The reason such a vanishing trace glows so brightly is the host itself: the crystal is a dense lattice of optically active Gd³⁺, and energy absorbed anywhere in the Gd³⁺ sublattice migrates to the rare Eu³⁺ sites. The whole crystal acts as an antenna for its own impurities.

    The switch: 13 nm of tuning flips the emission color

    Using the same tunable deep-UV source I built for my GeO₂ photodetector measurements — third-harmonic generation of a Ti:sapphire laser — we swept the excitation from 236 to 270 nm and recorded the emission at each step. The result is a continuous channel switch: at short excitation wavelengths the emission is dominated by the higher-lying Eu³⁺ ⁵D₂/⁵D₁ lines (491 nm and the 535–555 nm group); tune the excitation into the Gd³⁺ ⁶IJ absorption near 266 nm and that entire upper manifold collapses, leaving almost pure red ⁵D₀ → ⁷F₁,₂ emission at 590 and 612 nm.

    And the switch survives calibration. Re-plotting the same map with the emission normalized to the absorbed light and to the excitation power — using a NIST-traceable lamp calibration of the spectrometer — concentrates the story further: pumping the Gd³⁺ ⁶IJ resonance near 266 nm is by far the most efficient way to light up the red Eu³⁺ lines.

    The switch is quantitative and remarkably clean. The intensity ratio I₄₉₁/I₆₁₂ falls by more than an order of magnitude across the sweep, crossing unity near 251 nm, and its descending branch follows a single exponential (k = 0.124 nm⁻¹ — an e-folding every ~8 nm). In CIE color coordinates, the emission slides continuously from blue-green to orange:

    A single, nominally undoped crystal that retunes its visible emission color through excitation wavelength alone — no change in composition — is interesting in its own right for optical tagging and anti-counterfeiting and for ratiometric luminescence sensing. Here we used it as something else: a rapid, nondestructive probe of the host’s energy-transfer pathways.

    Proving who the emitter is

    Sharp lines at 491, 590, and 612 nm could in principle come from several rare-earth ions, so we mapped the full trace-impurity content of both crystals by LA-ICP-MS:

    The logic is a process of elimination: the two crystals have nearly disjoint impurity fingerprints, yet show the same sharp-line emission at identical wavelengths — so the emitter must be an impurity they share. Eu is the only visible-active one. The line positions match Eu-doped GdScO₃ from the literature, and the excitation behavior matches Gd³⁺ → Eu³⁺ sensitization.

    The practical payoff: screening substrates with light

    The two crystals differ most in a broad defect-emission band — strong in one, nearly absent in the other, and uncorrelated with the measured impurity content. Annealing one crystal at 1200 °C in air suppresses this band almost completely while the sharp Eu³⁺ lines survive, identifying it as an intrinsic, annealing-suppressible defect feature. The anneal also raises the Eu³⁺ ⁷F₂/⁷F₁ asymmetry ratio from 1.30 to 1.90 — the trace Eu acting as a built-in reporter of its improving local environment.

    Put together — excitation-selective photoluminescence, trace-element analysis, and annealing — the paper demonstrates a fast, nondestructive optical screen for scandate substrates: shine UV on a wafer and its glow reports both its energy-transfer pathways and its defect signatures, with direct relevance to substrate qualification for oxide electronics and to rare-earth scandates as excitation-tunable luminescent hosts.
