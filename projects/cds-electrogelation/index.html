    Electrochemical Sol-Gelation of CdS Quantum Dots

    Growing 3D quantum-dot gel networks with a voltage knob — phase- and size-dependent electrogelation of CdS QDs.

    Gels. Grown with volts.

    Watch zinc-blende CdS quantum dots assemble themselves into a three-dimensional network — live at the electrode, at 1.8 V.

    1.8 V vs. Ag/AgCl · 36 µM CdS@TGA in methanol · gel nucleating on the Pt anode

    Shadi Mirmohammadi — Materials Chemistry, University of Utah

    The idea: a voltage knob for building solids out of quantum dots

    Colloidal quantum dots are exquisitely tunable — their band gap and color follow their size — but as isolated particles wrapped in insulating ligands, they are hard to wire into anything macroscopic. Gelation solves this: cross-link the dots into a continuous, porous 3D network that keeps quantum confinement while allowing electronic communication, with enormous surface area for catalysis and sensing.

    Most QD gels are made by dumping in a chemical oxidant — simple, but it leaves residue and nucleates uncontrollably. This project uses electrochemistry instead: anodic oxidation of surface thiolates forms S₂²⁻ bridges between particles, so the gel grows directly on the electrode. The applied potential becomes a clean, tunable handle on oxidation strength — controlled kinetics, no chemical oxidant, and reversible, redox-active inter-particle bonds.

    Making the building blocks

    Zinc-blende CdS quantum dots are synthesized by hot injection, with size dialed in by the growth hold time and sulfur precursor amount — batches from 2.6 to 4.9 nm with narrow spectral linewidths (HWHM ≈ 12–13 nm), confirmed by XRD and TEM.

    To make the dots “gelable,” their native oleic-acid shell is swapped for thioglycolic acid (TGA) in basic methanol — turning them hydrophilic and exposing the surface thiolates that the electrode will oxidize. Absorption spectra before and after the exchange confirm the cores stay intact.

    Under the microscope

    The micrographs show exactly what a good gel precursor should look like: monodisperse ~4–5 nm particles, separated by thin ligand shells rather than fused, already arranging into short interconnected strings.

    Growing gels with volts

    The electrogelation cell is simple: 36 µM CdS@TGA quantum dots in methanol with 10 mM TBAPF₆ electrolyte, Pt working and counter electrodes, and an Ag/AgCl reference. Scanning the potential window from 1.4 to 2.4 V reveals a sharp onset: below ~1.6 V nothing happens; from ~1.7 V a translucent yellow gel nucleates and grows around the anode, with the applied potential setting the growth kinetics and gel morphology.

    Optically, the gels stay quantum: the excitonic peak barely moves after gelation (the QD cores are preserved), with only a slight red-shift from inter-particle coupling — no signature of bulk CdS.

    What the gel looks like inside: small-angle X-ray scattering

    SAXS turns the gel’s nanostructure into numbers. The scattered intensity follows a power law I(q) ∝ q−Df, and the fitted fractal dimension Df = 1.86 places the network in the reaction-limited (RLCA) regime: slow aggregation, strong inter-particle sticking, dense fractal clusters, and a more ordered — and more transparent — gel, with a correlation length of ~107 nm.

    Why phase matters — and what’s next

    Wurtzite and zinc-blende CdS present chemically different surfaces: wurtzite’s polar facets release Cd²⁺ about 3× more readily, promising faster oxidative gelation than the nonpolar zinc-blende surfaces studied here. Mapping this phase dependence — together with N₂ sorption (BET), TEM/SAXS, FTIR/XPS surface analysis, and optical spectroscopy across QD sizes — will show how gel structure connects to charge transport, and whether electrogelation runs diffusion- or reaction-limited across the phase diagram.
