    Photon Upconversion in CdSe–Anthracene Hybrids

    Turning two green photons into one violet photon with nanoplatelet–molecule hybrids — and why the geometry of a single carboxylic acid decides whether the energy flows.

    λ = 532 nm → 410 nm · upconversion

    Two green photons in, one violet out — at record efficiency.

    16% quantum yield · more than 2× prior reports

    Seyedeh Shadi Mirmohammadi, Farwa Awan, Tsumugi Miyashita, Todd D. Krauss, and Ming Lee Tang University of Utah & University of Rochester

    Poster presented at the 2nd International Symposium on Photochemistry & Functional Interfaces (ISPF2), New York, 2024 — view the poster (PDF).

    Making light climb uphill

    Photon upconversion fuses two low-energy photons into one higher-energy photon — green in, violet out. That lets a solar cell or photocatalyst harvest photons that would otherwise pass straight through, and gives bioimaging deep-penetrating excitation with visible readout. The workhorse mechanism is triplet–triplet annihilation (TTA), and it takes a three-part relay:

    1. a light absorber — here a CdSe nanoplatelet — soaks up 532 nm (2.34 eV) photons;
    2. a mediator covalently bound to the nanoplatelet surface accepts the triplet by Dexter transfer (TET1);
    3. a free emitter in solution — 9,10-diphenylanthracene (DPA) — collects the triplet (TET2), and when two DPA triplets meet, TTA launches one of them to the singlet, which emits at 410 nm (3.06 eV).

    In the Tang lab, hybrid CdSe–anthracene nanostructures built on this scheme have reached record TTA upconversion efficiencies around 16% — more than double prior reports — by tuning quantum-dot size and ligand structure. This poster pushes the platform in a new direction: replacing the quantum dots with nanoplatelets.

    Why nanoplatelets?

    CdSe nanoplatelets (NPLs) are quantum-confined in only one dimension — their thickness, controlled to the atomic layer (5.5 monolayers here). That has three attractions for upconversion: no size polydispersity smearing the spectra, enhanced exchange interactions that favor the dark (triplet-like) exciton driving Dexter transfer, and large absorption cross-sections for capturing light.

    One molecule, three geometries

    The mediator in this study is anthracenecarboxylic acid (ACA) — the carboxylate anchors it to the NPL surface, the anthracene relays the triplet. But where the carboxylic acid attaches to the anthracene changes the electronic coupling between the platelet and the aromatic core. We compared all three isomers — 1ACA, 2ACA, and 9ACA — as molecular wires between the same 5.5 ML CdSe NPLs and the same DPA emitter:

    What we measured

    For each isomer we titrated the ligand-to-nanoplatelet ratio and measured absorption, upconverted emission, and upconversion quantum yield (UPQY) under 532 nm excitation with 3 mM DPA in toluene at room temperature:

    The isomer ranking came out unambiguous: 9ACA (0.43%) > 1ACA (0.1%) > 2ACA (0.03%) — an order of magnitude between best and worst, from nothing but the position of one carboxylic acid. ATR-IR confirmed all three bind through the carboxylate (a shared 1280 cm⁻¹ signature, with a 1420 cm⁻¹ band distinguishing the 1ACA/2ACA binding mode from 9ACA’s).

    The takeaway

    Attachment geometry acts as a gate on triplet transfer: the same molecule, anchored through a different ring position, spans an order of magnitude in upconversion yield. But even the best isomer leaves most of the nanoplatelets’ absorbed energy on the table — the triplet population reaching DPA is small. The path forward is engineering the interface itself: controlling nanoplatelet stacking and optimizing the ligand-to-nanoplatelet ratio to boost the first, platelet-to-molecule hop that currently limits the relay.
