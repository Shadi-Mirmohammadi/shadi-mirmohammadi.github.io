    10 kV β-Ga₂O₃ Photoconductive Switches

    Switching 10 kV with a near-infrared laser — impurity-engineered Fe:β-Ga₂O₃ photoconductive semiconductor switches.

    Hunter Ellis, Imteaz Rahaman, Afrouz Taherian, Seyedeh Shadi Mirmohammadi, Botong Li, Michael A. Scarpulla, Berardi Sensale-Rodriguez, and Kai Fu Department of Electrical & Computer Engineering, University of Utah

    Manuscript ready for submission (2026).

    The idea: switching 10,000 volts with a laser pointer’s wavelength

    Photoconductive semiconductor switches (PCSSs) are optically triggered high-voltage switches — compact, fast, and with almost no off-state leakage — of interest for pulsed-power systems, ultrawideband radar, and grid-scale power electronics. Ultrawide-bandgap (UWBG) semiconductors like β-Ga₂O₃ (Eg ≈ 4.8 eV) are ideal hosts because blocking voltage scales with critical field. But there’s a catch: switching a UWBG material with band-to-band absorption requires deep-ultraviolet light, and high-power, fast UV sources are scarce.

    The workaround is impurity engineering. Fe doping introduces deep acceptor states ~0.8 eV below the conduction band of β-Ga₂O₃, so an ordinary 808 nm near-infrared laser can photoionize electrons from the Fe states into the conduction band — sub-bandgap optical triggering that keeps the material’s full high-voltage blocking capability. (It’s the same Fe:β-Ga₂O₃ photoconductivity that powers my optically tunable THz metasurface — one material platform, two very different devices.)

    The device and the experiment

    The switch is built directly on an Fe-doped β-Ga₂O₃ substrate (0.01% Fe, 650 µm thick), encapsulated in high-thermal-conductivity epoxy to suppress air breakdown at the electrode edges and help spread heat. A continuous-wave 808 nm laser — beam-expanded, iris-controlled, and power-monitored — illuminates the device while it holds off up to 10 kV from a high-voltage supply. Transient switching is read out across a 50 Ω load on a fast oscilloscope, with a thermal camera and temperature-controlled stage tracking self-heating throughout.

    What we found

    • It switches. Under 808 nm illumination the device transitions cleanly from a high-resistance off-state to a conductive on-state, at every bias from 1 to 10 kV.
    • It’s linear. Photocurrent grows approximately linearly with optical power — the switch operates in the linear photoconductive regime (no avalanche multiplication), where the carrier population simply tracks the absorbed photons.
    • On/off ratio > 15 at 10 kV bias and 2.5 W optical power, with switching amplitude enhanced by field-assisted carrier transport at higher bias.
    • The trade-off shows up on cue. At high optical power and high field, the on-state develops pronounced fluctuations: Joule heating thermally ionizes more electrons from the relatively shallow Fe states (an Arrhenius process), which raises the current, which heats the device further — a positive electrothermal feedback loop. Millisecond-scale fall times reflect the Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ charge-cycling kinetics of carrier recapture.

    Why it matters

    The study maps the central design tension of impurity-engineered UWBG switches: shallow impurity levels buy optical accessibility (cheap NIR lasers) at the cost of thermal stability, while deeper mid-gap levels (Cr, Co, Ir, Ni, Cu) promise stability but demand higher photon energies. These results — efficient near-infrared triggering at 10 kV alongside quantified electrothermal limits — give concrete guidance for choosing the impurity, the operating field, and the thermal design of future β-Ga₂O₃ pulsed-power switches.
