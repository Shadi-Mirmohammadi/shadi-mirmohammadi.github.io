    Engineering Spin States in Si QD–Organic Hybrids

    Can chemistry program a quantum dot's spin? Time-resolved EPR reveals that Si quantum dots hand their anthracene partners a fully spin-polarized triplet.

    ν = 9.5 GHz · microwave

    Chemistry that programs a quantum dot's spin.

    spin-polarized triplets · TR-EPR with Argonne

    Mandefro Y. Teferi, Sanoop Mambully Somasundaran, Shadi Mirmohammadi, Ming Lee Tang, Jens Niklas, and Oleg G. Poluektov Argonne National Laboratory & Department of Chemistry, University of Utah

    Submitted to the Journal of the American Chemical Society (2026).

    The question optical spectroscopy can’t answer

    Quantum dot–molecule hybrids are one of the most promising platforms for harvesting long-lived triplet excitons — the workhorses of photon upconversion, photocatalysis, and singlet fission. Silicon QDs are especially attractive: nontoxic, earth-abundant, and covalently functionalizable. But nearly everything known about triplet energy transfer (TET) in Si QD–molecule conjugates comes from optical measurements — photoluminescence and transient absorption — and light cannot see spin. Whether a triplet formed by ordinary spin–orbit intersystem crossing, by charge recombination, or by direct energy transfer is invisible to a photodetector.

    Time-resolved electron paramagnetic resonance (TR-EPR) can tell the difference. This work is the first TR-EPR study of triplet energy transfer in Si QD–organic hybrids.

    The system: three molecules, three linkers

    The conjugates pair 2.65 nm dodecane-capped Si QDs with three anthracene derivatives, covalently bound through different bridges (~7 acceptors per QD): 9-ethylanthracene (9EA) through a flexible single bond, 9-vinylanthracene (9VA) through a π-conjugated C=C linker, and 9,10-bis(phenylethynyl)anthracene (BPEA), a larger, more conjugated chromophore. Crucially, the 480 nm excitation can only be absorbed by the QD — any triplet that appears on the molecule must have been delivered by the dot.

    The surprise: the triplet arrives already spin-polarized

    TR-EPR at 20 K shows the molecular anthracene triplet unambiguously — including the formally forbidden half-field (ΔmS = 2) transition, a fingerprint of a molecular triplet. But the electron spin polarization is fully emissive: every line radiates microwaves instead of absorbing them, meaning the sublevels are populated top-down, p(T₊₁) > p(T₀) > p(T₋₁).

    Neither of the conventional triplet-formation mechanisms can do that — spin–orbit intersystem crossing and radical-pair recombination both produce mixed absorptive/emissive patterns. The only explanation is that the Si QD’s own dark exciton is born with its T₊₁ level overpopulated, and Dexter transfer delivers that polarization to the molecule intact. The quantum dot doesn’t just donate energy — it programs the spin state of its molecular partner.

    Reading the triplet’s wavefunction from its spectrum

    The zero-field-splitting parameter D acts as a molecular ruler: it measures how far apart the two unpaired electrons live. Simulating the spectra gives D = 2085 MHz for 9EA — essentially identical to free anthracene (2184 MHz), so the triplet stays localized on the chromophore behind its single-bond linker. The π-conjugated vinyl bridge of 9VA drops D to 1775 MHz — the triplet delocalizes across the linker (and 9VA also shows the strongest transfer signal). BPEA’s extended conjugation spreads the triplet furthest, D = 1600 MHz. DFT calculations of the triplet spin densities reproduce the trend quantitatively.

    Why it matters

    The result reframes Si QDs from passive light absorbers into spin-programmable triplet donors: the linker chemistry sets where the triplet lives, and the QD’s exciton physics sets its spin polarization. That combination — chemically tunable delocalization plus built-in non-Boltzmann polarization — is exactly what hybrid materials need for photon upconversion, spintronics, and quantum information applications.

    My role

    I contributed the chemistry side of the collaboration in Prof. Ming Lee Tang’s lab: synthesizing the Si QDs and covalently functionalizing them with the anthracene derivatives via air-free hydrosilylation, together with Sanoop Mambully Somasundaran. The EPR measurements and spectral simulations were performed by Mandefro Teferi, Jens Niklas, and Oleg Poluektov at Argonne National Laboratory. This is the work behind my poster at the 2025 Conference on Excited State Processes (ESP-2025), which received the Best Poster Award.
